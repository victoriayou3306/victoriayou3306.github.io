Mechanical

To avoid reinventing the wheel, we chose to use an open source, 3D printable robot called Arctos. We had ~2.5 months until EOH 2025, so it was a big challenge of sourcing parts from approved vendors, 3D printing parts, and managing the assembly process itself. The goal for the first EOH was just assembly and demonstration of concept. We planned to continue the project long-term to build a functional 3D printer for one of the Illinois Student Makerspaces. I won't get into the Arctos base design too much, as their website explains much of it in detail.

As expected, 3D printed gearboxes lead to a LOT of backlash. Most of it is coming from the two 150:1 Cycloidal gearboxes. Clearly this would need to be improved if we wanted to actually 3D print with the robot arm.

There were also ~700 fasteners, as well as many subsystems that were not designed for (dis)assembly. We also had to make a few changes to electrical components, requiring redesigns for mounting and housing without restricting the range of motion. *No shade to the Arctos project, as it is much easier to nitpick an existing design than it is to create one from scratch.

We ended up assembling and disassembling so many times that Sergio and I could look at any part and tell you where it went, what bolts were needed, when it was printed, what we wanted to improve about it, and basically its whole life story.
