Intro//Yap

This project took the majority of my freshman/sophomore spring semesters.*Easily counted double digits in all-nighters, multiple 60+ hour weeks, lots and lots of class/homework skipped... (sorry professors). This began under ASME@Illinois Product Development Committee. The goal was to 3D print a 6-axis robotic arm to be used for nonplanar 3D printing, and then manufacture multiple for simultaneous printing. I was fortunate enough to have serial robotics and 3d printing experience and was chosen as one of the project leads at the beginning of the project. Logistical issues were inevitable, and the first semester (2.5 months) of the project pretty much had a team of 4 people and a time-crunched dream. Literally everything that could have went wrong did. Wrong electrical parts were ordered from alternate vendors (they needed to be department-approved) when rushing tight deadlines, parts got lost in the receiving office, parts were out of stock, Amazon shipped us a faulty part, and my laptop somehow blew up. *I think my mishaps/frustrations with this project could be an essay in itself. But this isn't the place for that unfortunately. So the goal for the second year was to try to mitigate these unfortunate series of events.

I ended up taking a Co-Op in the Fall, so I wouldn't be there when the team was expanding. This meant I needed to start organizing my thoughts in a legible way, instead of keeping everything in my head or my unsaved txt file. I tried my best to help on the administrative end to maximize everyone's efficiency. While my co-lead Sergio and I could recite the parts and next steps in our sleep, organization was huge for the team to actually make progress.

Mechanical

To avoid reinventing the wheel, we chose to use an open source, 3D printable robot called Arctos. We had ~2.5 months until EOH 2025, so it was a big challenge of sourcing parts from approved vendors, 3D printing parts, and managing the assembly process itself. The goal for the first EOH was just assembly and demonstration of concept. We planned to continue the project long-term to build a functional 3D printer for one of the Illinois Student Makerspaces. I won't get into the Arctos base design too much, as their website explains much of it in detail.

As expected, 3D printed gearboxes lead to a LOT of backlash. Most of it is coming from the two 150:1 Cycloidal gearboxes. Clearly this would need to be improved if we wanted to actually 3D print with the robot arm.

There were also ~700 fasteners, as well as many subsystems that were not designed for (dis)assembly. We also had to make a few changes to electrical components, requiring redesigns for mounting and housing without restricting the range of motion. *No shade to the Arctos project, as it is much easier to nitpick an existing design than it is to create one from scratch.

We ended up assembling and disassembling so many times that Sergio and I could look at any part and tell you where it went, what bolts were needed, when it was printed, what we wanted to improve about it, and basically its whole life story.
